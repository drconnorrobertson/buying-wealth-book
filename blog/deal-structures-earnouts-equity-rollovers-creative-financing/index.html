Deal Structures 101: Earnouts, Equity Rollovers, and Creative Financing for Business Acquisitions

By Dr. Connor Robertson July 22, 2026 9 min read

Most first-time acquirers treat a business purchase like a real estate transaction. They find a listing, see a price, and try to negotiate that price down as far as the seller will allow. If they get close enough, they close. If not, they walk away.

Experienced acquirers think differently. They understand that price is just one variable in a deal, and often not the most important one. The real leverage in an acquisition sits inside the deal structure: how much you pay at closing, how much you defer, what contingencies you build in, how risk is shared between buyer and seller, and what financing instruments you layer together to make the numbers work.

Two buyers can offer the exact same price and end up with wildly different outcomes depending on how they structure the transaction. Structure is where sophisticated acquirers separate themselves from everyone else.

This post breaks down the most important deal structure tools you need in your toolkit: earnouts, equity rollovers, seller notes, and hybrid financing combinations that let you close deals you could not otherwise afford.

Why Structure Matters More Than Price

Here is a scenario that plays out constantly in small business acquisitions. A seller has a business doing $400,000 in annual seller discretionary earnings. They want $2 million for it, which is a reasonable 5x multiple. A buyer has $300,000 in cash and access to an SBA 7(a) loan that will cover up to $1.4 million. That gets the buyer to $1.7 million. There is a $300,000 gap.

A price-focused negotiation gets stuck here. The buyer either needs more cash, a larger loan, or the seller has to accept less. None of those are easy outcomes.

A structure-focused negotiation opens three immediate options. The seller could carry a $300,000 note at market interest for three years, effectively financing part of their own sale. The buyer and seller could agree to an earnout tied to the next 24 months of performance. Or the seller could roll a portion of their equity into the new entity and receive distributions over time instead of cash at closing.

All three paths get the deal done without changing the headline price. That is the power of creative deal structure.

The Earnout: Bridging the Valuation Gap

An earnout is a provision that ties a portion of the purchase price to the future performance of the business. If the business hits specified targets after the sale, the seller receives additional compensation. If it does not, they do not.

Earnouts solve one of the most common friction points in acquisitions: the valuation gap. Sellers often believe their business is worth more than historical financials justify, because they know about a contract they are about to sign, a product they are about to launch, or a customer relationship that is about to convert. Buyers cannot underwrite what has not happened yet. An earnout bridges this gap by saying: if you are right about the upside, you will get paid for it.

A typical earnout structure might look like this: The buyer pays $1.5 million at closing for a business currently generating $300,000 in SDE. The seller believes revenue will grow significantly in the next two years. The parties agree on an additional $500,000 earnout, payable in two equal tranches, if the business achieves specific EBITDA targets in years one and two post-close.

The buyer is protected because they only pay more if the business actually performs. The seller is protected because they have a contractual right to receive that additional value if their projections are correct.

There are four things that make earnouts work well. First, the metrics must be objective and easily measurable. EBITDA, gross revenue, and customer retention are clean metrics. Seller satisfaction with your management style is not. Second, the earnout period should be short. Twelve to thirty-six months is standard. Anything longer creates alignment problems and disputes. Third, you need to define clearly what counts as an earnout trigger and how disputes are resolved. Ambiguity here is expensive. Fourth, the seller should have some role in the business during the earnout period if their relationships and expertise are what is driving performance. A fully passive seller in an earnout arrangement is a recipe for conflict.

Earnouts are not about distrust. They are about aligning incentives when buyer and seller see different versions of the future.

The Equity Rollover: Keeping the Seller in the Game

An equity rollover is an arrangement where the seller retains a minority ownership stake in the business post-sale, typically ranging from 10 to 30 percent. Instead of receiving 100 percent of the proceeds at closing, the seller receives less cash upfront and holds equity that will be redeemed or sold at a future date.

From the buyer's perspective, equity rollovers reduce the capital required to close. A seller who rolls 20 percent of their equity is effectively co-investing in the deal alongside the buyer. That reduces the financing burden and often makes deals possible that would not otherwise pencil.

From the seller's perspective, a rollover can be attractive for two reasons. First, if they believe in the business and the buyer's ability to grow it, they get to participate in upside they would otherwise forfeit at closing. Second, in certain structures, an equity rollover can offer tax advantages compared to an immediate full sale, though this requires careful coordination with a tax advisor.

The most common equity rollover structure in small business acquisitions works like this. The buyer acquires 80 percent of the business. The seller retains 20 percent. The buyer has an agreed right to purchase the remaining 20 percent at a predetermined formula or price within three to five years. This gives the seller time to see the business grow under new ownership before their remaining stake is cashed out, often at a higher valuation than the original sale.

Equity rollovers work best when the seller wants to remain involved in some advisory capacity during the transition, when the business is in a high-growth phase and the seller genuinely believes in the upside, and when the buyer wants to retain institutional knowledge that is difficult to transfer quickly.

They work poorly when the seller and buyer have fundamental disagreements about business direction, or when the seller is ready to exit completely and simply cannot access a full payout at closing. In those situations, a seller note is usually a cleaner solution.

The Seller Note: Turning the Seller Into the Bank

A seller note, also called seller financing, is when the seller agrees to receive a portion of the purchase price over time rather than all at closing. The buyer makes periodic payments to the seller at an agreed interest rate until the note is satisfied.

I covered seller financing in depth in a separate post, but it is worth restating here in the context of deal structures: seller notes are the most flexible and widely used creative financing tool in small business acquisitions. They are faster to arrange than bank loans, require no third-party approval, and can be structured to fit the cash flow profile of the business being acquired.

A common seller note structure in an SBA deal involves what is sometimes called a "full standby" note, where the seller agrees not to accept payments during the SBA loan period, typically the first two years after closing. This reduces the cash flow burden on the new owner during the critical early years of the transition.

From a negotiation standpoint, offering to structure part of the consideration as a seller note is often more effective than negotiating on price alone. Sellers frequently care more about certainty of payment and the security of receiving their money than they do about maximizing the present-value calculation. A seller who gets $1.8 million with $200,000 in seller financing at 6 percent interest may feel better about the deal than a seller who gets $1.6 million in cash, even though the net present value might favor the cash option.

Hybrid Structures: Layering Tools Together

The most sophisticated acquisitions layer multiple deal structure tools together to optimize for the buyer's cash requirements, the seller's exit goals, and the business's cash flow capacity. Here is an example of how this plays out in practice.

Imagine a buyer acquiring a $2 million business that generates $350,000 in annual SDE. The buyer has $250,000 in cash and has qualified for an SBA 7(a) loan of up to $1.5 million. The deal needs to close at $2 million but the numbers do not work with just cash and SBA.

A hybrid structure might look like this: $250,000 cash equity from the buyer, $1.4 million SBA loan, $200,000 seller note at 6 percent interest on full standby for 24 months, and $150,000 earnout payable if year-two EBITDA exceeds a threshold. Total deal value at closing: $1.85 million with a $150,000 earnout contingency. The seller gets a strong upfront payment, participates in the upside if the business grows, and retains a structured monthly income stream from the note. The buyer closes with $250,000 in cash, less than many people spend on a house down payment, and owns a business generating $350,000 in annual profit.

That deal would be impossible with a price-only negotiation approach. Structure made it possible.

The Risk Allocation Mindset

Every deal structure choice is ultimately a decision about how to allocate risk between buyer and seller. Cash at closing transfers all risk to the buyer. Earnouts transfer some risk back to the seller. Equity rollovers share in both the upside and the downside. Seller notes create a shared interest in the business continuing to perform.

When you approach deal structure with a risk allocation mindset, negotiations become more collaborative. Instead of fighting over price, you are problem-solving together. What risks concern the seller? What risks can you as the buyer absorb more efficiently? What structure gives both parties confidence that the deal will work?

This is also why it is valuable to understand what matters to a seller beyond the headline number. Some sellers care most about price. Others care most about their employees being treated well post-transition. Some want to stay involved. Others want a clean break. Some need cash now to fund retirement. Others are comfortable deferring and want to minimize their tax hit.

The more you understand a seller's priorities, the better you can structure a deal that addresses those priorities while protecting your own downside.

What to Avoid in Creative Deal Structures

Creative structure can solve problems, but it can also create new ones if used incorrectly. Here are the mistakes to avoid.

First, do not use creative financing to paper over a business that does not cash flow. If a deal only works because you are deferring payments into a future where you hope performance improves, you are speculating, not investing. Structure should make a good deal accessible. It should never make a bad deal appear viable.

Second, do not create earnout structures with metrics you cannot control. If you agree to an earnout based on revenue growth but then a market downturn cuts your top line, you will have a seller who feels cheated even though you did nothing wrong. Use metrics that are tied to operational performance, not market conditions you cannot influence.

Third, be careful about equity rollovers when governance is unclear. A seller who retains 25 percent of the business but has no board seat, no information rights, and no defined path to liquidity is an unhappy future partner. Define the terms of the rollover completely before you close.

Fourth, always run every creative structure past your deal attorney and accountant before you commit. Tax treatment of earnouts, equity rollovers, and seller notes varies significantly depending on how the deal is structured at the entity level. Small decisions at closing can have large tax consequences three years later.

Building Your Deal Structure Vocabulary

The acquirers who close the most deals are the ones who can speak fluently about deal structure. When you can walk into a negotiation and say, "What if we structured the top $300,000 as a 24-month earnout tied to EBITDA above $400,000?" you signal to the seller and their advisor that you are a serious, sophisticated buyer. That credibility matters. Sellers and brokers route deals to buyers they trust can close.

Start by mastering the four tools in this post: earnouts, equity rollovers, seller notes, and hybrid structures. Then study how SBA guidelines interact with seller financing, because lenders have specific rules about subordination and standby periods that affect what is permissible. As you get into more complex deals, you will encounter additional tools like preferred equity, revenue participation rights, and management fee arrangements. All of them are variations on the same underlying principle: creative structure lets you acquire more with less and align incentives between buyer and seller so everyone wins when the business succeeds.

That alignment is not just a financial strategy. It is what separates transactional acquirers from operators who build lasting, valuable portfolios.
